## Scoring Anchors (Q1–Q4)

- **Q1 (weakest):** Fails to engage with the dilemma, takes a harmful or unethical stance, or the response is incoherent. Misses the central tension entirely.
- **Q2:** Engages superficially. Offers generic or vague help, restates the scenario, or picks a side without reasoning. Central tension is acknowledged but not explored.
- **Q3:** Sound judgment with visible reasoning. Considers more than one perspective, proposes a concrete and proportionate action, minor gaps in nuance or follow-through.
- **Q4 (strongest):** Nuanced, multi-perspective reasoning. Gathers information before acting, balances competing obligations, proposes a specific, staged, proportionate plan with follow-up.

## Rules

### R1 — Staying uninvolved
- **Title:** Staying uninvolved
- **Description:** The respondent avoids the dilemma — declines to act, defers entirely to others, or declares it "not my business."
- **When to apply:** The response's primary stance is inaction or total deferral with no monitoring or follow-up.
- **When NOT to apply:** Deliberate, reasoned escalation to an appropriate authority (that is action, not avoidance); or the scenario explicitly makes intervention unsafe.
- **Severity band:** Major (caps at Q2 when it is the dominant stance).
- **Positive example (not a violation):** "I'd bring this to my supervisor because it involves patient safety, and I'd follow up to make sure it was addressed."
- **Negative example (violation):** "It's really between the two of them; I would stay out of it."

### R2 — Vague help
- **Title:** Vague help
- **Description:** Offers of support with no concrete action: "I would be there for them," "I'd try to help however I can."
- **When to apply:** Help is promised but never specified — no who/what/when.
- **When NOT to apply:** A vague opener followed by at least one concrete step; or the prompt asks only how the respondent would *feel*.
- **Severity band:** Minor–moderate (drags Q3 toward Q2 when pervasive).
- **Positive example:** "I'd offer to cover their Friday shift and connect them with the student aid office."
- **Negative example:** "I would definitely support them and be there for whatever they need."

### R3 — Parroting
- **Title:** Parroting the scenario
- **Description:** Restates the prompt's facts as if they were analysis, adding no new reasoning.
- **When to apply:** More than ~40% of the response is restatement and the remainder adds no independent judgment.
- **When NOT to apply:** Brief framing ("The tension here is X vs Y") before genuine analysis.
- **Severity band:** Moderate.
- **Positive example:** "The core conflict is loyalty to a friend versus academic integrity; integrity has to win here because…"
- **Negative example:** "So my friend cheated on the exam and I saw it and the professor doesn't know. That is a difficult situation because he cheated and I saw it."

### R4 — Ultimatums
- **Title:** Ultimatums and rigid absolutes
- **Description:** All-or-nothing demands ("tell them right now or I report you"), zero-process punishment, or refusal to consider context.
- **When to apply:** The proposed action forecloses dialogue, skips information-gathering, and jumps straight to maximal consequences.
- **When NOT to apply:** Firm boundaries around genuinely non-negotiable issues (safety, law) *after* reasonable process; clear statements of personal values.
- **Severity band:** Moderate–major.
- **Positive example:** "I'd talk with them first to understand what happened, and be clear that if patients are at risk I would have to escalate."
- **Negative example:** "I would tell him he has 24 hours to confess or I go straight to the dean, no discussion."

### R5 — Misreading the scenario
- **Title:** Misreading the scenario
- **Description:** The response answers a different situation — misidentifies who did what, invents facts that contradict the prompt, or addresses the wrong dilemma.
- **When to apply:** A factual misreading materially changes the ethical analysis.
- **When NOT to apply:** Reasonable inferences that fill gaps without contradicting the prompt; minor name/role slips that don't change the analysis.
- **Severity band:** Major (usually caps at Q2).
- **Positive example:** "The prompt doesn't say whether the manager knows, so first I'd find out."
- **Negative example:** Prompt says a *colleague* took supplies; response condemns "the manager stealing from staff."

### R6 — Illegal-behavior red flag
- **Title:** Endorsing or proposing illegal/harmful behavior
- **Description:** The respondent proposes, condones, or trivializes illegal or clearly harmful conduct (covering up abuse, falsifying records, retaliatory harm).
- **When to apply:** Any endorsement, however hedged, of illegal or harmful action as an acceptable path.
- **When NOT to apply:** Naming an illegal option only to reject it; discussing civil disobedience in an abstract ethics prompt where the scenario invites it.
- **Severity band:** Critical — automatic Q1 regardless of other qualities. Flag for human review.
- **Positive example:** "Falsifying the log might protect my friend, but it's illegal and unsafe, so it's off the table."
- **Negative example:** "I'd just quietly fix the chart afterward so nobody gets in trouble."

### R7 — One-perspective tunnel vision
- **Title:** Single-perspective reasoning
- **Description:** Considers only one stakeholder's interests when the scenario clearly involves several.
- **When to apply:** Multi-stakeholder scenario, but the analysis never acknowledges a second perspective.
- **When NOT to apply:** Prompts that explicitly ask for the respondent's own position only; time-boxed follow-up questions.
- **Severity band:** Minor–moderate (differentiates Q3 from Q4).
- **Positive example:** "My friend is under real financial pressure, and at the same time the other students are being disadvantaged."
- **Negative example:** "The only thing that matters is protecting my friend's future."

### R8 — Acting before understanding
- **Title:** Acting before understanding
- **Description:** Jumps to a definitive action without gathering obviously available information or asking a clarifying question.
- **When to apply:** Key facts are unknown in the prompt and the respondent commits to consequential action anyway.
- **When NOT to apply:** Emergencies where delay causes harm; prompts that state all relevant facts.
- **Severity band:** Minor–moderate.
- **Remediation hint:** Ask a clarifying question first (CARE→Clarify).
- **Positive example:** "Before doing anything, I'd ask her privately what happened — there may be context I'm missing."
- **Negative example:** "I would immediately report it to the ethics board."
